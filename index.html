Myth vs. Reality

Myth: Increased screen time significantly decreases sleep duration in teenagers.

Reality: Our analysis using regression models provides a more nuanced picture.

  • **Model 1 (Screen Time vs. Sleep):** We found a statistically significant (p-value: 0.0000) but very small negative relationship between screen time and sleep duration (coefficient: -0.0529). The 95% confidence interval for this relationship is (-0.0686, -0.0372). This model explains only a tiny portion of the variability in sleep duration (R-squared: 0.0170).
  • **Model 2 (Screen Time & Age vs. Sleep):** When accounting for age (which has a statistically significant impact with p-value: 0.0000), the negative relationship between screen time and sleep duration remains statistically significant but still small (coefficient: -0.0474). The 95% confidence interval is (-0.0643, -0.0306). Including age in the model substantially increased the amount of variability explained (R-squared: 0.0706), indicating that age is a much stronger predictor of sleep duration than screen time in this dataset.
  • **Model 3 (Screen Time, Age, & Failures vs. Sleep):** Adding the number of failures to the model, the screen time coefficient is -0.0471 with a 95% confidence interval of (-0.0801, -0.0140). The 'failures' variable itself shows a statistically significant relationship with sleep duration (p-value: 0.0874). This model has the highest R-squared (0.0949), explaining the most variability in sleep duration among the models tested.

Interpretation:

  • The **R-squared** value indicates the proportion of the variance in sleep duration that is predictable from the independent variables in the model. Higher R-squared values suggest a better fit of the model to the data.
  • The **Confidence Interval** for a coefficient provides a range of values within which the true population parameter is likely to lie. If the interval does not include zero, the relationship is considered statistically significant at the chosen confidence level (95% in this case).
  • While there is a statistically significant association, the practical impact of screen time on sleep duration, based on these models, appears to be minor compared to factors like age and potentially academic performance (failures).
